Procuring IT equipment for donor-funded projects in Southern Africa requires more than finding the lowest price. Donors and auditors expect transparent sourcing, valid warranties, and documentation that demonstrates value for money.

## Start with specifications, not brands

Write your technical requirements first. Processor class, memory, storage, networking standards, and warranty period. This gives suppliers a fair basis to quote and protects you during audit.

## Require authorised supply channels

Grey market hardware may be cheaper upfront but creates warranty and audit risk. Insist on supply through authorised distributors with OEM documentation. Request distributor authorisation letters and manufacturer warranty registration details with your quote.

## Build an audit-ready procurement pack

For each purchase, retain:

- The original quote with itemised pricing and part numbers
- Purchase order and invoice
- Delivery note with serial numbers
- Warranty certificates and registration confirmation
- Import documentation where applicable

## Plan for lead times

Hardware sourced from European distributors typically requires 7 to 21 business days plus customs clearance. Build this into your project timeline and avoid last-minute procurement that forces compromise on specification or supplier.

## Use a single supplier where possible

Consolidating procurement through one supplier simplifies documentation, reduces coordination overhead, and typically improves pricing at modest volumes.
